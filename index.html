Bergen's Game of Life

The ones that listen too fast, too slow, or too loud fail — in three different, visible ways.

About the math (one click deep, as promised)

This is a variation on Conway's Game of Life where each live cell carries five continuous parameters instead of a single alive/dead bit. The parameters are the five receiver primitives of RPCS-1 (IMM Paper 9):

PrimitiveMeaning hereRange
TI — temporal integrationHow long a memory the cell averages its neighborhood over (EMA window)1–16 steps
SG — sensitivity / gainHow strongly it amplifies what it detects0.2–3.0
FT — filter thresholdMinimum neighborhood signal that registers at all0–0.6
UE — update eagernessLearning rate: how fast the cell re-tunes its own TI0–0.5
AR — anticipationA directional guess about the neighborhood's next move; correct guesses earn a small energy bonus±1

The environment coordinate is local entropy H: half spatial (binary entropy of the 8-neighborhood's alive fraction), half temporal (fraction of neighbors that flipped last step). The survival rule is the Matching Principle (Pred-09-5): a cell earns energy in proportion to how well TI ≈ 1/H holds — long memory in a calm world, short memory in a noisy one. Mismatch is scored symmetrically in log space.

Mismatched cells drift into the three failure modes of IMM Paper 11 (Theorem 11-2):

  • Oscillation — TI far below 1/H: the cell reacts to every flicker.
  • Freeze / rigidity — TI far above 1/H: the cell stops responding to real change.
  • Overload — SG × H too high: too much gain in too noisy a world drains energy directly.

Births inherit the neighborhood's mean TI (culture, not genetics) plus noise. The Learning slider scales every cell's UE; at zero, cells cannot re-tune and failure modes accumulate. Classic Conway (B3/S23) is included as the baseline mode for contrast.

Presets: Two worlds seeds a calm sparse half against a dense noisy half — the same learning rule drives TI to opposite targets on each side. Failure showcase plants three colonies with deliberately mis-set, non-learning receivers (UE = 0): one listening too fast, one too slow, one turned up too loud — each displays its failure color, then pays for it. Gliders seeds classic Conway gliders in baseline mode.

What this is and isn't: this simulation is an illustration of IMM/RPCS-1 concepts — an artistic sandbox, not evidence. Nothing that happens on this grid bears on whether any IMM claim is true. The Matching Principle is a registered prediction of the framework, not an established law.

Engine and unit tests are in this site's repository. The simulation is deterministic given a seed (mulberry32 PRNG).
